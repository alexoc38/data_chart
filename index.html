Black Immigrant Domestic Workers Endure Grave Conditions During COVID-19

By: Alex O'Connor
Nov. 12, 2021

Report shows how the intersecting identities of Black, immigrant, woman, and low-wage worker often translated to economic precarity during the height of the pandemic

A report from the Institute of Policy Studies in partnership with the National Domestic Workers Alliance highlights the vulnerabilities of a population employed in one of the lowest paid sectors in the country. Survey data looking at personal economic impact and health and safety risk was collected from undocumented and documented Black immigrants in New York, Miami-Dade, and Massachusetts. The National Domestic Workers Alliance is currently pushing for the passage of the Build Back Better bill in hopes to ameliorate poverty and employer mistreatment.

Isaac Jabola-Carolus is a PhD candidate in sociology at the CUNY Graduate Center whose labor research focuses on domestic workers. He provided his review of the report, “Large-scale survey data on domestic workers, especially immigrant and undocumented workers, is immensely difficult to gather. This report, "Notes from the Storm," represents a valuable effort to reach that population during a highly volatile time, capturing the widespread hardship experienced by Black immigrant workers during the pandemic. I am especially struck by the data on workers in Miami-Dade, an area where domestic worker research has been especially limited in the past.”

Data source: Notes from the Storm: Black Immigrant Domestic Workers in the Time of COVID-19, survey data collected May 19-June 6, 2020.
